## CORE THESIS

Banksy is a structured commercial joint venture, active 1998–2023. The enterprise operates through three distinct functional roles held by three distinct individuals:

**The Artist** — Lucy McKenzie (b. Glasgow, 1977). Trompe l'oeil painter and muralist. Hand-paints to approximate stencil appearance. Art directs shows in response to corporate and AoR input. Her participation in the pre-canonical collaborative network is documented. Her technical practice is the foundation of the Banksy graphic vocabulary.

**The Voice** — Kerri McKenzie. Oxford physics and philosophy graduate. HPS (History and Philosophy of Science) metaphysics fundamentality PhD, 2012. The written Banksy voice — author of the enterprise's conceptual and textual output including books, statements, and wall text. PhD completion concurrent with Round 2 capitalisation period.

**The Artist of Record (AoR)** — Damien Hirst. Corporate controlling stakeholder. Legal identity attached to the enterprise for contractual, commercial, and liability purposes. Controlling stake confirmed through the corporate structure. The AoR designation is distinct from both the Artist and the Voice.

The geographical split is approximately 1/5 English (Hirst, Bristol) and 4/5 Scottish (Lucy McKenzie, Kerri McKenzie, Bernie Reid, and others).

## GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS

**AP (Artist's Proof):** A number of works produced outside the numbered edition, conventionally reserved for the artist's personal use. In the Banksy system, the baseline AP structure is 22 per edition: 1 artist copy, 10 VIP allocation, 11 POW-retained inventory.

**AoR (Artist of Record):** The individual whose legal identity is attached to the Banksy enterprise for contractual, commercial, and liability purposes. Distinct from the Artist (who produces the work) and the Voice (who produces the written and conceptual output).

**COA (Certificate of Authenticity):** A document issued by Pest Control Office (PCO) authenticating a Banksy work. Works without a COA trade at a significant discount on the secondary market. The COA system is the enterprise's primary market control mechanism.

**PCO (Pest Control Office Limited):** The authentication and certificate-issuing body for Banksy works. Incorporated 2008. Acquired by POW in January 2020.

**POW (Pictures on Walls):** The print publishing and distribution arm of the Banksy commercial enterprise. Controls what is printed, in what quantities, at what prices, and through which channels.

**Secondary market:** The resale market for works after their initial sale. Price appreciation in the secondary market accrues to the seller, not the artist or publisher.

**Trompe l'oeil:** French, "deceives the eye." A painting technique that creates the illusion of mechanically reproduced or printed surfaces on a flat plane. A skilled trompe l'oeil painter can produce work visually indistinguishable from a screenprint or stencilled spray paint at normal viewing distance.

**VIP Round:** A pre-public allocation of signed works from a new edition, offered at a fixed price to a defined group of institutional investors and associates before retail release.

## Article 8 — The Print Bubble and Its Deflation
### The Market Architecture of a Managed Scarcity Operation, 2002–2024

---

The first thing to understand about the Banksy print market is that it was not a market in the ordinary sense. A market implies price discovery — buyers and sellers arriving at values through competition, information, and the aggregated judgments of independent actors. What the Banksy print operation built between 2002 and its peak years in the late 2010s was something structurally different: a **managed scarcity system** in which edition size, release cadence, authentication, resale advisory, and critical narrative were all controlled, either directly or through proxies, by the same small group of people.

To understand how that system inflated, and how it began to deflate, you have to understand the architecture before you can read the numbers. The numbers are real — the auction records are public, the corporate filings are public, the edition sizes are documented — but numbers without structure are just data. The structure is the story.

POW[^1] was the publishing and distribution arm. It controlled what was printed, in what quantities, at what prices, and through which channels. The unsigned editions were the mass market vehicle. The signed editions were the premium product. The APs[^2] were the invisible inventory — works held back from the public market that could be released, gifted, or deployed as needed. The secondary market[^3] ran on the signed editions and the APs, because those were the works that collectors believed were finite. The unsigned editions traded too, but at lower multiples, because the ceiling on their scarcity was always visible.

Between 2007 and 2012, the major auction houses[^4] opened dedicated urban and street art sale categories, and the Banksy secondary market acquired institutional legitimacy it had previously lacked. Prices that had been negotiated informally in back-channel dealer conversations were now public auction records. Public records create benchmarks. Benchmarks create perceived value floors. Perceived value floors attract buyers who are not primarily interested in the art.

This is the mechanism of any speculative bubble in a finite-supply asset class. The Banksy operation did not invent it. What it did was run it with unusual structural discipline — controlling edition sizes[^5] carefully enough that supply never visibly exceeded demand, timing releases to maintain price tension, and managing the authentication function[^6] in a way that gave the market confidence it was trading genuine scarcity rather than manufactured scarcity. The distinction, from the buyer's perspective, is everything. From the structural perspective, it is semantic.

The gift prints[^7] are instructive here because they sit outside the commercial edition structure and therefore reveal the underlying logic by contrast. Ice Cream Van 2009: fifty signed giclees, distributed to the Bristol Museum crew, no PCO certificate of authenticity. Rodeo Girl 2008: forty signed giclees, distributed to the Cans Festival crew, no PCO COA. Police Riot Van 2015: fifty-two signed, Dismaland crew, no PCO COA. These are personally dedicated works — some inscribed by name — that circulate entirely outside the authentication system. They are not edition works. They are gifts.

The reason this matters is that the authentication system — PCO — was not designed to capture all works. It was designed to capture the commercial editions and the APs that would trade on the secondary market. The gift prints were a parallel economy of personal obligation and crew loyalty that the commercial apparatus left deliberately alone. A gift print that enters the secondary market without a COA is either a problem or an opportunity, depending on who is holding it and what they need to prove.

The bubble's architecture becomes most legible when you look at what was built around it rather than inside it. In 2008 — the year the primary art market cracked under the weight of the financial crisis, the year Damien Hirst ran his Beautiful Inside My Head Forever sale at Sotheby's the week Lehman collapsed[^8] — two websites appeared that deserve examination as a pair.

Banksyexplained.com and banksy-value.com[^9] are not, in themselves, remarkable things. Explanatory websites and price-tracking websites exist for many artists. What is remarkable is their timing and their complementarity. An explanation infrastructure tells a buyer what a work means. A valuation infrastructure tells a buyer what a work is worth. You build both of these simultaneously when you are trying to convert a street art phenomenon into a stable investment-grade asset class. The explanation website provides the cultural justification. The valuation website provides the financial floor. Together they perform the function that a prospectus performs in a securities offering: they give the buyer a reason to believe the price is real.

In the same year, Moonstar Fine Arts Advisors LLC was incorporated in Florida.[^10] Incorporated 25 April 2008. The date places it at the beginning of the primary bubble's institutional phase. Its public profile through the peak years was minimal — a dormant entity, not a functioning advisory business with a visible client roster. It became named and visible at the point when attribution questions about the Banksy enterprise intensified and when buyers and their legal representatives needed something to point to as an institutional validator. It was administratively dissolved on 26 September 2025, as the legal environment around the enterprise became materially more complex.

Moonstar is a pass-through structure. Incorporated early enough to claim institutional longevity. Dormant publicly through the bubble years. Activated as a named liability vehicle when attribution became necessary. Dissolved as legal exposure increased. This is not a description of an art advisory business. It is a description of a corporate instrument designed to provide the appearance of independent validation while remaining operationally connected to the enterprise it was nominally advising.

The vertical integration completed in January 2020 when PCO was acquired by POW.[^11] The same week, MurderMe Limited — the Hirst print publishing vehicle — was renamed Prints and Editions Limited.[^12] Two things happened simultaneously: the authentication body for the Banksy secondary market came under the direct ownership of the primary market publisher, and the Hirst print operation was rebranded in a way that collapsed the distance between the Hirst and Banksy commercial print streams into a single visible corporate identity.

An authentication body owned by the publisher of the works it authenticates is not an independent verification service. It is a quality control function for the publisher's own inventory. The certificates it issues are not third-party attestations of provenance. They are manufacturer's marks. The market treated them as the former. The corporate structure confirms they were the latter.

The Girl with Balloon shredding at Sotheby's in October 2018 is the single most important event in the bubble's lifecycle, and it has been systematically misread as a prank.[^13] It was not a prank. It was a market recapitalisation. The Banksy secondary market had been showing signs of price fatigue by 2017–2018 — auction results were flatter, the growth curve that had sustained the investment narrative was moderating. A partially shredded work, renamed Love is in the Bin, converted a declining asset into a performance art event. It reset the price ceiling. It generated global media coverage that no advertising budget could have purchased. And it created a new category of Banksy work — the post-shredding piece — that carried a provenance story unlike anything else in the market.

The work sold for £18.6 million in 2021. The original signed edition had sold for £1.04 million at the 2018 sale. The shredding multiplied the value by a factor of approximately eighteen. Whether that multiplication was planned from the outset or was an opportunistic exploitation of an accident is a question the evidentiary record does not definitively resolve. What the record does confirm is that the enterprise had the structural capability to execute the conversion — the authentication, the rebranding, the provenance narrative — in a way that maximised the financial outcome.

The deflation, when it came, followed the broader post-pandemic correction in speculative asset classes.[^14] NFTs peaked in early 2022 and collapsed. Meme stocks peaked in early 2021 and corrected. Street art prints peaked in roughly the same window. The Banksy secondary market was not immune to the asset cycle that everything else was subject to, because it had been incorporated into that asset cycle as a deliberate investment vehicle.

The structural difference between the Banksy deflation and a pure speculative correction is that the vertically integrated apparatus gives the enterprise tools that a pure speculative vehicle does not have. It controls the authentication of new works entering the market. It controls the certification of existing works changing hands. It controls the interpretive narrative through affiliated channels. An enterprise that controls all three of these simultaneously has the capacity to manage a deflation rather than simply absorb it.

Managing a deflation means controlling the rate at which authenticated works enter the secondary market, maintaining the authenticity premium on certified works relative to uncertified works, and sustaining the critical narrative that justifies the premium. It does not mean preventing the deflation. It means shaping it.

The AP baseline structure is where the retained inventory question becomes concrete. The standard 22-AP allocation — 1 artist copy, 10 VIP allocation, 11 POW-retained inventory — means that for every public edition sold out at retail, POW held eleven APs in inventory. For Girl with Balloon, which had an unusually large AP run of 88 — four times the baseline — the POW-retained inventory was forty-four works. At 2021 peak secondary market prices, forty-four Girl with Balloon APs represent an asset position of considerable scale. That position did not evaporate in the deflation. It changed in value. The question of how and when those works were released into the market, and through which channels, is one the public record does not fully answer.

The Bomb Love prints carry a different AP signature: AP equals six, all POW-retained, no artist copy. This is a post-renegotiation structure — the artist's copy has been removed from the allocation. A print run where the AP stratum contains no artist copy is a print run where the artist has accepted a reduced stake in the retained inventory. That reduction was not accidental. It was the product of a contractual negotiation whose terms are reflected in the corporate structure of the enterprise from the May 2002 contractual lock forward.

What the print bubble was, in its fullest description, is a financial instrument built on top of a creative enterprise by people who understood both the creative and the financial sides well enough to engineer the instrument deliberately. The works were real. The street interventions were real. The cultural resonance was real. None of that requires the market architecture to have been organic or accidental.

The market architecture was not organic. It was built — edition by edition, AP allocation by AP allocation, authentication body by authentication body, valuation website by valuation website — over a period of twenty years by a small group of people with complementary skills in art production, corporate structure, and financial engineering. The bubble inflated because the architecture was good. It began to deflate because the architecture could not hold the price against the asset cycle indefinitely. And the deflation has been managed because the same architecture that built the bubble is still operational.

The Moonstar dissolution in September 2025. The BBAY Art dissolution in January 2026. The Ant and Dec High Court proceedings filed March 2026. These are not unrelated events. They are the leading edge of an unwinding that the architecture was designed to survive, but not to prevent.

## Article 9 — The Glasgow Peace Is Tough Omission
### What the Official Exhibition Record Does Not Say, and Why the Silence Is the Evidence

---

There is a principle in forensic investigation that is sometimes stated as an axiom and sometimes left implicit but is always operative: absence of evidence is not the same as evidence of absence, but a specific, targeted absence — an absence that cuts precisely along a line of structural interest — is evidence. It is evidence of a choice. Someone chose not to include the thing that is missing. That choice was made for a reason. The investigator's task is to establish what the reason was.

Glasgow Peace Is Tough took place in 2001.[^15] It was a group exhibition. It was curated by Bernie Reid.[^16] Among the participating artists was Lucy McKenzie,[^17] showing her own fine art work in a context that placed her directly inside the collaborative network from which the Banksy enterprise was simultaneously being structured. The show is documented. It happened. It is not in the Banksy Exhibition record.[^18]

That is the entirety of the primary fact. Everything else in this article is the explanation of why that fact matters and what it means.

The Banksy Exhibition record is not a neutral archive. It is a curated document — curated in the precise art world sense of the word, meaning that decisions were made about what to include and what to exclude, and those decisions reflect an intention. The intention, in the case of the BE, is to establish the canonical history of the Banksy enterprise: where it showed, with whom, under what circumstances, building from the earliest Bristol interventions through the major institutional shows to the present.

A show omitted from the BE is not a show the enterprise forgot.[^19] The enterprise does not forget shows. It controls its own historical record with considerable care — the care of an operation that understands its provenance narrative is a financial asset as well as a cultural one. Every show in the BE adds value to the works associated with it. Every omission removes a connection the enterprise has decided it does not want made.

Glasgow Peace Is Tough was omitted because including it would place Bernie Reid, Lucy McKenzie, and the Banksy enterprise in the same room, under documented circumstances, at the precise moment — 2001, one year before the May 2002 contractual lock[^20] — when the collaborative structure that became Banksy was being assembled. The omission cuts the pre-canonical collaborative record. That is its function.

To understand the weight of this omission, you need to hold two things simultaneously: the chronology of the enterprise's formation, and the evidentiary significance of the Reid credit on the Untitled 06 edition.

Bernie Reid is not a marginal figure in the Banksy record. He is credited on the Untitled 06 edition[^21] — a closed edition of ten works sent from Switzerland, one of the most anomalous objects in the entire Banksy print corpus. An edition of ten, sent from outside the standard distribution chain, with a Scottish artist credited, is not a footnote. It is a structural anomaly that points toward the pre-canonical period with unusual directness. Reid's credit on that edition is not decorative. It is a remnant of an arrangement that predates the formal commercial architecture — an arrangement in which Reid's role was acknowledged because the formal apparatus for obscuring such acknowledgments had not yet been fully constructed.

Reid as lead curator of Glasgow Peace Is Tough 2001 is the institutional context for that arrangement. He was not a peripheral associate who happened to appear in a group show. He was the curatorial authority of a documented exhibition that placed him in an organising relationship with the artists — including Lucy McKenzie — who constitute the creative core of the Banksy thesis argued in this Codex.

Lucy McKenzie's practice is built on trompe l'oeil.[^22] This is not a casual observation about her work. It is the technical foundation of the identification argument. A painter who specialises in producing surfaces that appear to be something other than hand-painted — surfaces that read as mechanical reproduction, as print, as stencil — is a painter who possesses precisely the skill set that the Banksy graphic vocabulary requires. The works associated with the Banksy name that appear to be stencilled spray paint are not always what they appear to be. Some of them are hand-painted to approximate the appearance of stencilled spray paint. This is trompe l'oeil applied to street art iconography. It is a technically demanding practice. It is Lucy McKenzie's practice.

The 2005 unique — the oil-painted bouquet — is the one point in the public record where the artist's hand is confirmed operating in an explicitly painterly mode rather than a stencil mode. It is not a coincidence that the one confirmed painterly work in the Banksy record sits adjacent to the period when Lucy McKenzie's fine art career was establishing its mature form. The Strathclyde artist identity[^23] thesis does not rest on this single data point. But this single data point does not contradict it.

The question of how Glasgow Peace Is Tough entered the investigative record is worth addressing directly, because the answer illustrates something important about how buried institutional history resurfaces.

It resurfaces through physical holdings. The Untitled 06 edition — ten works, sent from Switzerland, Reid credited — is a closed object. It exists in the world. People who hold it know what it says. The Reid credit is not hidden inside a corporate filing or derivable only through inference from Companies House records. It is printed on the work. Anyone who owns a work from that edition, or has examined one carefully, has seen it. The investigation began with that credit and worked backward to the institutional context that explains it.

Glasgow Peace Is Tough is that institutional context. It is the documented public event that places Reid in an organising role relative to McKenzie at the relevant moment. Without the show, the Reid credit is an anomaly without an explanation. With the show, the Reid credit is a remnant of a collaboration whose full scope the enterprise subsequently chose not to acknowledge.

The three-function model of the Banksy enterprise — Artist, Voice, Art of Record[^24] — requires that each function be held by a distinct individual whose skills and biography fit the requirements of that function. The AoR thesis assigns that role to Damien Hirst on the basis of his corporate position, his financial stake, and his capacity to manage the commercial and legal architecture. The Voice thesis assigns that role to Kerri McKenzie[^25] on the basis of her intellectual formation, her academic work in philosophy of science and metaphysics, and the structural parallels between her documented thinking and the conceptual content associated with the Banksy name. The Artist thesis assigns that role to Lucy McKenzie on the basis of her technical practice, her documented presence in the collaborative network, and the physical evidence encoded in the works themselves.

Glasgow Peace Is Tough is the hinge point for the Artist thesis. It is where Lucy McKenzie's institutional presence in the pre-canonical network is publicly documented. Its omission from the BE is the enterprise's acknowledgment — through silence — that including it would make the connection visible to anyone examining the record carefully.

The doubling motif in the work is relevant here because it suggests the artist was not passively anonymous but actively encoded.[^26] The Untitled 1 and 2 paintings. The Olympia photographs. Works that contain within themselves the formal structure of a two-person creative relationship. These are not coincidences of composition. They are a signature — a signature that operates below the threshold of the authentication system, below the threshold of the corporate record, at the level of the image itself. An artist who knows their identity has been structurally suppressed and who continues to work within that suppression will find ways to mark the work that are visible to the people who need to see them and invisible to the apparatus that is managing the suppression.

The Glasgow omission is the corporate-level version of that encoding. The enterprise could not include the show without making the network visible. It could not exclude it without creating an absence precisely shaped like the thing it was trying to hide. The investigator's task is to notice that the absence has a shape.

Robin Gunningham[^27] absorbed a decade of identification effort. He was the shape the enterprise offered to people who were looking for a shape. The tabloid stories, the Bristol geography, the sub-par hand spray work visible in early documentation — all of it pointed toward a person who was real enough to be found and insubstantial enough to exhaust the investigation. While the Gunningham hypothesis held the field, the actual collaborative record sat in plain sight: in the Reid credit on the Untitled 06 edition, in Lucy McKenzie's documented participation in Glasgow Peace Is Tough, in the corporate filings that trace the financial architecture back to a structure that Gunningham could not have built and did not need.

The Gunningham thesis required the enterprise to have been built by a Bristol graffiti artist with no documented corporate experience and no institutional connection to the fine art market. The corporate record describes something different: a joint venture structured by people with access to gallery infrastructure, print production facilities, auction house relationships, and a legal architecture sophisticated enough to manage a twenty-year commercial operation across multiple jurisdictions.

These two descriptions cannot both be true. The corporate record is not speculative. It is filed with Companies House. It is public. It predates this investigation by two decades.

Glasgow Peace Is Tough is Article 9 of this Codex for a specific reason. It sits at the end of the BE/BV block — the block that establishes the enterprise's own account of its history — as the final demonstration that the enterprise's account of its history is a managed document. Every article in the block before this one has examined what the enterprise built and how it operated. This article examines what the enterprise chose to forget.

What it chose to forget is the room where the collaboration was visible before the apparatus for making it invisible had been completed. The show happened in 2001. The contractual lock came in May 2002. The window between those two dates is the window in which Glasgow Peace Is Tough falls. It is the last moment at which the collaborative record was allowed to be public.

The omission is the evidence. The silence has a shape. The shape is Lucy McKenzie standing in a Glasgow gallery in 2001, showing her own work, in a show curated by the man who would later be credited on the most anomalous edition in the Banksy print record, one year before the formal architecture of suppression was put in place.

## Appendix 1 — The Gift Prints
### A Parallel Economy of Personal Obligation Outside the Authentication System

---

The commercial edition structure of the Banksy enterprise — signed editions, unsigned editions, APs, VIP rounds, PCO certificates of authenticity — is extensively documented because it was designed to be documented. It is the part of the operation that interfaces with the institutional art market, the auction houses, the galleries, the collectors who need provenance chains and legal title. The documentation is the product. You cannot sell a Banksy print at Sotheby's without the paperwork. The paperwork requires the system.

The gift prints[^28] sit entirely outside that system. No PCO certificate.[^29] No edition number. No retail channel. Personally signed. Personally dedicated by inscription in most cases. Distributed directly to individuals who worked on the productions with which the prints are associated. They are gifts in the precise sense — transfers of value outside the commercial mechanism, carrying obligations and acknowledgments that the commercial mechanism is not designed to carry.

There are four documented gift print sequences in the evidentiary record. Each one repays careful examination, because together they describe not just a distribution pattern but a relationship map — a map of who the artist acknowledged, how, and at what moments.

The medium matters.[^30] All three crew gift sequences — Ice Cream Van, Rodeo Girl, Police Riot Van — are giclees. Not screenprints. A giclee is an archival inkjet print produced from a digital file. It can be reproduced at will from the same file, subject only to the decision of the person who controls the file. The scarcity of a giclee edition is entirely a function of the decision not to print more. There is no mechanical constraint on further production, as there is with screenprinting, which requires physical setup and carries inherent process limits.

This means the gift print editions are not scarce in the way the commercial screenprint editions are scarce. They are scarce because the enterprise chose to produce a finite number and distribute them personally rather than commercially. That choice is itself informative. It says: these works are outside the market. They are not for sale. They are for the people who were in the room.

The Bristol Museum sequence[^31] is the largest: fifty signed giclees distributed to the crew of Banksy vs. Bristol Museum, 2009. The show drew over 300,000 visitors. It was the largest Banksy institutional show to that date and one of the most significant events in the enterprise's public history. The crew who made it happen received personally signed prints. No certificates. No edition records in the standard commercial sense. The contrast between the show's institutional scale and the intimate, off-record nature of the gift distribution is deliberate.

The Cans Festival sequence[^32] is earlier: forty signed giclees for the Leake Street crew, 2008. The Cans Festival was a different kind of production — more distributed, less institutionally framed than the Bristol Museum show — but the gift print logic is identical. People who organised and built the event received personally signed works from the artist. Off the commercial record. Outside the authentication system.

The Dismaland sequence[^33] is the latest and the most logistically complex. Fifty-two signed giclees for the production crew of a temporary bemusement park involving 58 artists, constructed in a disused lido, open for five weeks. The production crew for an undertaking of that scale is not a small group. Fifty-two prints distributed across that crew is a significant act of acknowledgment — one that the commercial record does not capture because it was designed not to capture it.

The fourth sequence is different in kind from the first three and requires separate treatment.[^34] The Girl with Balloon and Morons Sepia double-sided works — edition of 8, spray and screenprint, 2007 — are not crew gifts in the production sense. They are not associated with a specific show or event. They are personally dedicated works, at least one of which is inscribed to "Simon."

Simon Durban is the structurally most plausible recipient of a personally inscribed work from the artist in 2007.[^35] He was POW director from 2005. He was PCO founding director from 2008. He held five simultaneous directorships in the enterprise's corporate cluster and resigned all five on the same day — 7 November 2019 — which is the real operational wind-down date of the enterprise's first-generation corporate structure. A man who held that position for that long, and who left in that manner, is a man to whom the artist had obligations that the commercial record does not acknowledge. An inscribed double-sided print in an edition of eight is the form those obligations took in 2007.

The farewell gift hypothesis[^36] characterises the entire 2007 double-sided sequence as works produced at a moment of structural transition — gifts that encode relationships the enterprise was in the process of formalising and, in some cases, closing. The edition of eight is small enough to be a personal distribution. The double-sided format — two works in one object — encodes the same doubling logic that appears elsewhere in the artist's practice at this period.

What the gift print record establishes, taken as a whole, is the existence of a relationship map that runs parallel to and underneath the commercial record. The commercial record tells you what was sold, to whom, at what price, with what documentation. The gift print record tells you who the artist felt obligated to, whose labour was being acknowledged, whose loyalty was being rewarded, and whose departure was being marked.

These are different kinds of information. The commercial record is about the market. The gift print record is about the people.[^37] In an enterprise structured around anonymity — in which the artist's identity is the central protected asset — the gift print record is one of the few places where the human relationships inside the enterprise leave a physical trace that was not designed to be erased.

The COA system[^38] was designed to control the authenticated secondary market. It was not designed to capture the gift prints because the gift prints were not intended for the secondary market. When a gift print does enter the secondary market — as some inevitably do, as crews disperse and financial circumstances change — it circulates in a stratum where provenance is established through personal testimony rather than institutional certification. The enterprise retains control of the authenticated tier. The gift print tier operates independently. This is not an accident of design. It is the design.

The investigative significance of the gift prints is threefold. First, they confirm the existence of a production crew — a group of people who built the shows, installed the works, managed the logistics — whose relationship with the artist was personal and acknowledged, and whose existence the commercial record renders largely invisible. Second, they provide a partial relationship map for the enterprise's operational history that cross-references with the corporate record: the shows that generated gift print sequences are the shows around which the corporate structure was most actively configured. Third, the Simon inscription on the 2007 double-sided work provides a direct link between the personal acknowledgment register and the corporate officer register — a link that the enterprise's official record does not make.

The gift prints are Appendix 1 of the Codex because they are the first place in the evidentiary record where the human architecture of the enterprise becomes visible in physical objects rather than corporate filings. The filings tell you the structure. The prints tell you the relationships. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.

## Appendix 2 — VIP Rounds
### The Syndicate Structure Encoded in the Distribution Record

---

The AP baseline structure of the Banksy print operation is where the enterprise's financial architecture becomes most legible, because the numbers are fixed and public and the logic they encode is derivable from the edition records without inference. When you see a consistent 22-multiple across editions of different scales — Police Kids at 22, Bomb Love at 44, NOLA at 66, Girl with Balloon at 88 — you are looking at a unit of account. The 22 is not an aesthetic choice. It is a structural one.

Twenty-two APs per baseline unit.[^39] One artist copy. Ten VIP allocation. Eleven POW-retained inventory. The VIP allocation of ten is the number of positions in the Round 1 syndicate. The eleven POW-retained works are the publisher's deferred inventory — works held back from the public market that could be released, gifted, or deployed as market conditions required. The artist copy is the artist's personal stake. These three numbers, multiplied across editions of different scales, describe an enterprise that was managing not just a print market but a defined group of financial participants whose interests were encoded in every edition it produced.

The VIP round[^40] is the mechanism through which that defined group received its preferential allocation. Before an edition reached retail — before the unsigned works went to the website, before the signed works went to the gallery waiting list — a fixed number of signed works were offered to the syndicate at a price set below anticipated secondary market value. The difference between the VIP price and the secondary market price is the financial return on syndicate membership. It is not a gift. It is a structured benefit attached to a defined position within the enterprise.

Two VIP rounds are documented with sufficient specificity to anchor the analysis. A third round structure is inferrable from the post-schism edition record. Each round corresponds to a phase of the enterprise's development and to a specific capitalisation requirement.

### Round 1 — Love Hurts, 2012

Love Hurts is the Round 1 anchor.[^41] Sixteen signed APs, never offered to the public retail market. Shown at Art in Streets[^42] — the major institutional survey of street art history at MOCA Los Angeles — in a context that maximised cultural legitimacy while maintaining financial exclusivity. The allocation structure encodes the cap table[^43] directly: fifteen syndicate positions plus the artist, the AoR, the gallery, and the production company. A signed AP that never reaches retail is not a collector's object in the ordinary sense. It is an equity instrument issued to a defined group of participants at the moment of the enterprise's first formal institutional capitalisation.

The Pitt-Jolie participation[^44] as Round 1 backers is consistent with the scale and timing of their documented Banksy acquisitions and with the profile of the participant group implied by the allocation record. Their presence in the syndicate is not the point. Their presence confirms that the Round 1 syndicate was composed of individuals with sufficient financial capacity and cultural adjacency to make the instrument worth issuing — and worth holding.

Baron Cohen's position in this structure is different.[^45] He is characterised here as sweat equity — contribution of creative and network capital rather than financial investment. His involvement in Exit Through the Gift Shop, his position within the London creative network that includes the enterprise's founding partners, and his absence from the financial allocation record taken together describe a participant who was inside the structure without a financial stake in the VIP round sense. Sweat equity and financial equity are different instruments. Both are real.

### Round 2 — Dumbo, 2014

The Dumbo allocation[^46] is Round 2. Twenty-five signed works, VIP price £35,000, never publicly offered, 10/15 split.[^47] The price point — £35,000 per work, multiplied across 25 positions — represents a capitalisation event of £875,000 at VIP price alone, before any secondary market appreciation. The timing places it immediately before the Dismaland and Walled Off Hotel productions,[^48] both of which required upfront capital at a scale the standard print revenue stream could not have funded without a dedicated capitalisation mechanism. Round 2 is that mechanism.

The LV hypothesis[^49] — a principal associated with the LVMH group as Round 2 anchor investor — is consistent with the scale of the allocation, the price point, and the pattern of luxury brand adjacency visible in the enterprise's commercial relationships during this period. The hypothesis remains inferential. What is not inferential is the allocation structure itself, which describes a single anchor position of sufficient scale to underwrite the production requirements of both major works that followed.

The post-schism edition record carries the marks of the renegotiation in the signature structure.[^50] Control Your Weapon and NOLA Girl are signed by Hirst as AoR rather than by the artist. The King Robbo sequence[^51] — the works acknowledging and restoring Robbo's tag after the 2009 overpainting incident — is the visible evidence that the artist needed to be re-engaged with the street credibility infrastructure at a moment when the enterprise's positioning had deteriorated. You do not make a public gesture of acknowledgment toward a respected figure in the graffiti community unless you need something from that community. The gesture is the proof of the need.

The NAPALM/SERPENTINE box set — edition of 50, 29 APs[^52] — carries the clearest post-schism signature anomaly. Twenty-nine APs only resolves if Hirst is excluded from the standard allocation. The baseline 22 would include an AoR position. Twenty-nine APs with no AoR share is a contractual statement: the artist re-engaged on terms that excluded the AoR from participation in this specific work. That exclusion was a condition. The condition was accepted. The 29 is the record of the negotiation.

What the VIP round record establishes is that the Banksy enterprise was not managed as an art operation with financial side effects. It was managed as a financial operation with an art production function. The distinction is not semantic. An art operation produces works and sells them. A financial operation structures the distribution of those works to generate defined returns for a defined group of participants, with the public market serving as the price-discovery mechanism that validates the private allocation.

The public market — the retail buyers, the auction bidders, the secondary market collectors — provided the price floor that made the VIP allocation valuable. Without a functioning secondary market producing ascending price records, the VIP round is worth nothing. The enterprise needed the public market to work. It also needed to remain structurally invisible to the public market. The anonymity of the artist was the mechanism that maintained that invisibility — not just the anonymity of the individual, but the anonymity of the structure.

The VIP rounds are the place in the record where the structure is least anonymous. Sixteen signed APs that never reached retail. Twenty-five signed works at £35,000 each distributed to a defined group before public release. A cap table encoded in an edition allocation. These are not the actions of a street artist. They are the actions of a managed investment vehicle that happened to produce street art.

## Corporate Timeline

### A Chronological Record of the Enterprise's Formation, Operation, and Unwinding

---

The timeline below is a chronological record of the corporate and operational events that constitute the evidentiary spine of this investigation. It is not a history of Banksy artworks. It is a history of the enterprise that produced them. Every date is drawn from public filings and public records. Where the record moves from fact to inference, that shift is marked. The pattern of events is the argument. Any single event is just a filing.

---

### Era 1 — Foundation: 1997–1998

Something is being assembled before anyone knows what it will become. The brand name is not fixed. The artist designation is not formalised. The corporate infrastructure is arriving ahead of the product it will eventually house.

In August 1997 a flyer circulates for Walls on Fire at Bristol Docks. It is signed "Banks." One letter short of the name that will eventually be worth hundreds of millions of pounds. The infrastructure, meanwhile, is already moving. On 3 September 1997, three weeks after that flyer, **Turtleneck Ltd** is incorporated. The share register reads like the guest list for a particular kind of late-nineties British cultural party: Damien Hirst with 50 shares from a Devon address, Keith Allen with 50 from NW3, Alex James with 50 from WC2, Joe Strummer with 50 from Somerset, Simon Jonathan Kennedy with 50 from W10 — identity still unconfirmed in the public record — and Karen Jayde Milner with a single share that will subsequently be transferred to Hirst, giving him effective majority control. SIC 82990: other business support service activities not elsewhere classified. The founding partnership is encoded in the share register before the enterprise has a name to trade under.

One year passes. Then in the space of three weeks in October and November 1998, the operational layer arrives in sequence. On 22 October 1998, **Identity Crisis Ltd** is incorporated at a Darlington address with SIC code 58190 — publishing. The name does not survive a fortnight. On 11 November 1998 it becomes **Pro-Actif Communications Ltd**, still active to the present day. The rename within the first twenty days of incorporation is the earliest instance in the record of the enterprise's characteristic practice: name an entity something, then change it before anyone has noticed what it was called. Three weeks after Pro-Actif, on 12 November 1998, **Electric Earth Records Ltd** is incorporated. This is the Artist's vehicle. Music-adjacent, unassuming, exactly the kind of entity that passes unremarked in a filing search. The founding layer is complete.

---

### Era 2 — Expansion: 2002–2008

The pre-canonical period closes with a contractual event that leaves a trace in the corporate record. In May 2002, Electric Earth Records is renamed **Underwater Records Ltd**. The rename is administrative. What it marks is not. May 2002 is the month of the contractual partition that narrows the artist designation — the decision that confirms Lucy McKenzie as the hand and Damien Hirst as Artist of Record. Bernie Reid's canvas editions produced before this date are grandfathered. Everything after runs under the formalised terms. The vehicle's name tracks the enterprise's phase like a geological marker.

The commercial infrastructure assembles through the middle years of the decade. **Pictures on Walls Ltd** becomes the primary print editions vehicle operating from Maddox Street W1S 1QP. The Lazarides gallery relationship formalises when Laz is appointed director of LazInc in 2004, the same year Durban joins the POW board. In February 2006, Durban is appointed secretary of LazInc — his Watford address appears on the filing. Durban is now administratively bridging POW and LazInc simultaneously. The two operations share an operational administrator. In 2006, Warren is fired following the Santa's Ghetto debacle. Eine is fired in the same period after the eBay backdoor print scandal. The enterprise enforcing its exclusivity terms, and making visible that it has them to enforce.

**Barely Legal** in Los Angeles in 2005 takes the enterprise transatlantic. The Kate Moss editions are produced. The print programme expands. The market is building toward something.

On 13 November 2007 Underwater Records becomes **GBFTCU Ltd** — the second and final rename of the Artist's vehicle. The name is a pointed statement. The Artist renegotiates to remain for the full project lifecycle. Commercial maturation and the vehicle's final name arrive together.

January 2008: **Pest Control Office Ltd** is incorporated at Old Gloucester Street WC1N 3AX. Durban is founding director. Holly Cushing joins as Secretary. The authentication function, previously informal, is now a separate legal entity with its own address. PCO and POW are distinct corporate structures sharing personnel. July 2008: **Paranoid Pictures Film Company Ltd** incorporated at Maddox Street. Durban director. The film production vehicle for what will become Exit Through the Gift Shop. August 2008: the BBAY property cluster core entities begin incorporating. A shadow secondary market infrastructure, running at the SIC 68100 real estate code, assembling quietly alongside the primary market apparatus.

Then September 2008: **Beautiful Inside My Head Forever** at Sotheby's. £111 million. The market peak. The enterprise at the top of its first arc.

One month later, in October 2008, the Lazarides share buyback completes. The financial mechanism of the separation. On 25 April 2008 — six months earlier, anticipating the restructuring — **Moonstar Fine Arts Advisors LLC** had been incorporated in Florida. A pass-through advisory vehicle incorporated early enough to claim institutional longevity, dormant publicly through the bubble years, present in the record as a named vehicle should attribution ever become necessary.

---

### Era 3 — Schism and Consolidation: 2009–2018

The separation completes administratively in March 2009 when Durban resigns from LazInc. The share buyback was the financial event. This is the operational confirmation. Durban, as the enterprise's internal administrator, is no longer performing that function for the Lazarides vehicle. The bridge is down.

The schism's root is structural. Hirst chose monopoly over emeritus. The multi-artist diversification plan — Lucy McKenzie's walk-away deal, the condition on which the original terms had been accepted — is abandoned. Hirst goes all-in on himself and Banksy. The LA print run in 2009 and 2010 is the proximate cause of the artist's withdrawal: POW jumped the gun on print quality and violated the artist's final say over the editions. On 16 July 2010, **GBFTCU Ltd is dissolved**. The Artist's corporate vehicle is wound down. The loan-out structure ends. The enterprise attempts to operate without its artist.

**King Robbo** in 2011 is the market's verdict on that attempt. A flop. The artist is needed. The enterprise cannot sustain the brand without Lucy McKenzie's hand.

The re-engagement comes in 2012. **Evil Under the Sun** for the Fiorucci Foundation. The condition on which the artist returns is a new capitalisation round. Round 1 had closed around Love Hurts in 2012 — sixteen signed APs, never public, the Art in Streets context — with fifteen syndicate members plus artist, Artist of Record, gallery, and Paranoid Pictures. The cap table encoded in the edition structure. Pitt-Jolie as Round 1 backers. Round 2 follows in 2014: **Dumbo**, twenty-five signed, ten/fifteen split, never public, VIP price thirty-five thousand pounds. Louis Vuitton as the hypothesised Round 2 anchor. The round funds **Dismaland** in 2015, the **Walled Off Hotel** in 2017, and Hirst's Wreck of the Unbelievable — for which the edition-of-two with five artist proofs encodes Lucy McKenzie's participation in a project nominally attributed to Hirst.

Concurrent with the Round 2 capitalisation period, Kerri McKenzie completes her Oxford HPS metaphysics fundamentality PhD in 2012. The written Banksy voice completing her academic formation while the enterprise restructures around the re-engagement terms.

In 2013 Holly Cushing becomes PCO Director — her role at the authentication body formalised at its highest level. In July 2015, **Outsiders and Execution Prints dissolve on the same day** — the Lazarides network following the same synchronised dissolution practice as the main enterprise, two entities wound down with one operational decision. October 2018: **Girl with Balloon shreds at Sotheby's**. The shredder pre-installed in the frame. The enterprise's most public brand event.

---

### Era 4 — Vertical Consolidation: 2019–2020

Ten weeks. Two events. The first-generation structure ends.

In October 2019, **BBAY Art Ltd** is incorporated at Old Gloucester Street WC1N 3AX — the PCO address. SIC 90040: artistic creation. The shadow secondary market vehicle and the authentication body now share a postbox. On **7 November 2019**, Simon Durban resigns simultaneously from POW, PCO, Paranoid Pictures, Dismaland, and GDP. Five entities. One date. A director winding down involvement in multiple entities over time is an ordinary transition. A director resigning from five entities on the same calendar day is a negotiated exit — a single legal event, not five separate decisions. 7 November 2019 is the real operational wind-down date of the enterprise's first-generation structure. The post-board vehicle is Positive Accounting Solutions Ltd. The name is legible as a description of function.

Ten weeks pass. On **29 January 2020**, MurderMe Ltd is renamed **Prints and Editions Ltd** at New Street Square. The same week, **PCO is acquired by POW**. The vertical consolidation is complete. Authentication and commercial operation are a single corporate structure. The Hirst print vehicle has a new name. The enterprise has folded into itself.

On 6 July 2021, **Turtleneck Ltd is dissolved** — the founding entity of September 1997 formally closed, two years after the structural wind-down it preceded by twenty-two years.

---

### Era 5 — Controlled Retreat: 2021–2026

The peripheral layer dissolves. The legal exposure materialises.

In February 2023 a Jersey entity, **BBAY Eaton Place**, is registered on the UK overseas register. Nineteen days later, on 20 February 2023, **BBAY Properties Ltd** is renamed **Stamford UK Holdings** at its Millbank Tower address. Establish the receiving vehicle, then rename the originating vehicle to break the nominal connection. Both moves are public record. The nineteen-day gap is the operational signature.

From September 2024 to February 2025, Lucy McKenzie's **Super Palace** exhibition runs at Z33, Belgium. A Bacon gambling den mural. An Epstein/American Psycho mural. A Trans-Siberian panorama. The show closes February 2025. The High Court proceedings are filed the following month.

In February 2025, **Rivers Threadneedle** — the most recent Lazarides vehicle — enters liquidation. The Lazarides network reaching its terminus.

Then the dissolution cluster arrives in sequence. **Moonstar Fine Arts Advisors LLC** is administratively dissolved in Florida on 26 September 2025 — incorporated April 2008, dissolved September 2025, dormant publicly for seventeen years between those dates. **Slap Dash Inc** (Companies House 10212238), the Holly Cushing-associated vehicle at SIC 90040, dissolves the same month. **BBAY Art Ltd** dissolves in January 2026 — five weeks before the Ant and Dec High Court proceedings become public record. In March 2026 the proceedings are filed before Judge Iain Pester: twenty-two art transactions, fraud allegations, Party X and Company X unnamed.

The seven-month sequence from Moonstar's dissolution to the High Court filing is not a coincidence of calendar. It is a controlled retreat. The peripheral advisory and secondary market management layer winding down as the legal exposure it was designed to manage arrives anyway.

---

### Three Partition Events

The corporate record resolves around three moments that divide everything before from everything after.

**May 2002** narrows the artist designation and locks the core structure. Everything before is pre-canonical. Everything after runs under the formalised terms.

**October 2008 – March 2009** removes the enterprise's street-credibility infrastructure and gallery front. The Lazarides separation is complete when Durban's administrative bridge between POW and LazInc is taken down.

**7 November 2019 – January 2020** ends the first-generation structure and folds the enterprise into a single corporate body. The Durban resignations are the operational wind-down. The PCO acquisition is the structural completion.

The dissolution cluster of 2025–2026 is not a fourth partition. It is the arrival of consequences that the structure was always designed to defer.
